Your Business Model Blueprint:
Mastering the Magic Triangle

Stop focusing solely on products—true success lies in redefining how your company creates and captures value.

The Harsh Truth

Small tweaks won't save you. Real innovation demands systemic overhaul—a change so deep that ignoring it makes established companies disappear.

The Four Pillars of Value

Every business model is comprised of four integrated dimensions, often called the 'magic triangle'. Before attempting innovation, clearly map out your existing structure using these four pillars:

1

WHOThe Customer

Define precisely which customer segments are relevant for you and which you will or will not address. Remember, the customer is always at the heart of every business model—there are no exceptions.

Action: Who are our core segments? Who influences their purchasing decisions?
2

WHATThe Value Proposition

Clarify the products and services you offer and exactly how you satisfy your target customers' needs. Your offering solves customer problems.

Action: What value or benefit do we create that differs significantly from competitors?
3

HOWThe Value Chain

Detail the processes, activities, resources, and capabilities required to produce and deliver your value proposition. This is how you organize internal operations and partnerships.

Action: How are we currently producing our offering? Which key activities rely on our core competencies?
4

VALUEThe Profit Mechanism

Determine how the model is financially viable, including cost structures and revenue-generating mechanisms.

Action: What are the main sources of income, and what are customers truly willing to pay for?
Dimension Description / Focus Key Questions to Ask
WHO?
(Customer) 		Target Customer Segment: Precisely define the relevant customer segments you will or will not address. The customer is always at the heart of the business model. Who are your most important customers and stakeholders? Who influences your customers?
WHAT?
(Value Proposition) 		The Offering: The products and services provided and how they satisfy the target customers' needs. What customer problem are you attempting to solve? How do your offerings differ from competitors'?
HOW?
(Value Chain) 		Internal Architecture: The processes, activities, resources, and capabilities required to produce and deliver the value proposition. What key resources and competencies do you need? Who are your most important partners?
VALUE?
(Profit Mechanism) 		Financial Viability: Cost structures and revenue-generating mechanisms that explain how the business model is financially viable and generates profit. What are your main sources of income? What are customers truly willing to pay for? What are your most significant cost drivers?

The Rule of Two for Breakthrough Innovation

A critical insight: merely changing your product or process is insufficient.

The Golden Rule

Innovation of a business model requires modifying at least two of these four WHO-WHAT-HOW-VALUE dimensions simultaneously.

If you only change the value proposition (WHAT), you usually only achieve product innovation, not a true business model revolution.

Case Study: Rolls-Royce's Power by the Hour

Before

WHAT: Selling jet engines

VALUE: One-time large purchase

→

After

WHAT: Selling flying hours (retaining ownership)

VALUE: Constant revenue streams + reduced maintenance costs

Result: This simultaneous reconfiguration of two dimensions created a formidable competitive advantage.

Key Takeaway

Before you can innovate, you must understand your current business model architecture. Map your four pillars honestly. Then, when you're ready to innovate, remember: change at least two dimensions simultaneously, or you're not really innovating your business model—you're just tweaking your product.
