The 90% Rule:
How to Generate Breakthrough Ideas

Contrary to the "Einstein Myth," commercial success rarely comes from ideas no one has ever had before.

The Shocking Truth

A staggering 90% of all successful business model innovations simply recombine existing ideas and concepts from other industries.

You don't need to be Einstein. You need to be a master of creative imitation and recombination.

Use Patterns as Your Innovation DNA

Adopt the concept of 55+ successful business model patterns as the "LEGO bricks" needed to build or innovate your existing model. Innovation is about systematic creative imitation and recombination, not pure copying.

Think of business model patterns as proven templates that have worked across different industries. Just like architects don't reinvent how to build a door, you don't need to reinvent business models from scratch.

Three Core Strategies for Innovation

Strategy Action / Goal Practical Application
Transfer Apply a successful pattern from an unrelated industry to your own. This uses others as blueprints, allowing you to avoid their mistakes. Take a pattern (e.g., Razor and Blade from razors) and apply it to a new product context (e.g., coffee capsules).
Combine Merge two or three distinct patterns to create a highly unique and difficult-to-imitate business model. Combine Razor and Blade, Lock-In, and Direct Selling (as Nespresso did). This increases planning complexity but builds stronger competitive barriers.
Leverage Re-use a successful model you already developed for another product range, capitalizing on experience and synergies. Apply the business model used for product A (e.g., Nestlé Nespresso) to a new product line, like product B (e.g., Nestlé Special.T or BabyNes).

Master Class: How Nespresso Combined Three Patterns

The Nespresso Triple Pattern Strategy

Pattern 1: Razor and Blade

Sell coffee machines at low/moderate prices → Generate recurring revenue from high-margin capsules

Pattern 2: Lock-In

Patented capsule system → Customers can ONLY buy Nespresso capsules (until patents expired)

Pattern 3: Direct Selling

Bypass retail stores → Control customer experience, collect data, maximize margins

Result: A fortress business model so robust that even when patents expired, Nespresso maintained market leadership through brand loyalty and ecosystem control.

Choosing Your Ideation Approach

Two fundamental principles guide how you select and apply business model patterns. Choose based on your problem definition and creative capacity.

Feature Similarity Principle Confrontation Principle
Selection Criterion Business model patterns found in similar industries. Business model patterns from extreme, unrelated industries.
Motto Lose your familiarity with what you know. Become familiar with what you don't know.
Recommendation Best for innovation projects with a specific, defined problem formulation. Best for innovation projects with an open or hazy problem formulation.
Primary Advantage Better structured and suitable for creativity beginners. Forces you to break out of thought patterns and uncovers undreamed-of innovation potentials.
Primary Disadvantage May only partially demolish thought patterns; risk of remaining stuck with known issues. Requires a high degree of creativity and is cognitively more demanding.

The Power of Confrontation: The "McDonald's Test"

Force Radical Thinking

To force radical, non-incremental ideas, intentionally confront your current business model with scenarios from completely unrelated industries.

Ask yourself extreme questions like:

  • "How would McDonald's manage our business?"
  • "How would Tesla approach our industry?"
  • "How would Netflix disrupt us?"
  • "How would Amazon enter our market?"

Example: Automotive Supplier Meets McDonald's

Question: "How would McDonald's manage our complex automotive components business?"

Initial reaction: "That's absurd. McDonald's makes burgers, we make precision components!"

Breakthrough insights after forcing the analogy:

  • McDonald's: Extreme standardization → Could we modularize our components?
  • McDonald's: Franchising model → Could we license our manufacturing processes?
  • McDonald's: Global supply chain mastery → Could we redesign our logistics?
  • McDonald's: Predictable quality anywhere → Could we create plug-and-play components?

Key Rule: Don't give up too early! Building analogies to extreme patterns is cognitively taxing, but necessary to hit the "Eureka!" moment.

Your Turn: The Pattern Challenge

3-Step Pattern Innovation Exercise

Step 1: Pick a Pattern

Browse the 60 business models on this site. Pick one that's completely unrelated to your industry.

Step 2: Force the Analogy

Spend at least 30 minutes asking: "How would this pattern work in MY business?" Don't quit when it feels impossible—that's when breakthroughs happen.

Step 3: Combine Patterns

Pick two more patterns. What happens if you combine all three? Can you create a Nespresso-level fortress?

Key Takeaway

Stop waiting for lightning-strike genius. Innovation is systematic creative recombination. Transfer proven patterns from other industries. Combine multiple patterns to create fortress-like defensibility. And when you're stuck, ask the extreme question: "How would [insert wildly different company] run our business?" The answer will surprise you.
