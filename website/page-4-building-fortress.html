Building the Fortress:
Ensuring Coherence

A revolutionary idea is only a hallucination until it is meticulously integrated.

The Integration Imperative

"It is much harder for rivals to copy an array of interlocked activities (a coherent business model) than to copy a single product feature." — Michael Porter, Strategy Pioneer

The Ideation phase produces many raw ideas. The Integration phase molds promising ideas into a cohesive whole—a fortress that competitors can't breach. This often requires iterative changes until everything locks together perfectly.

The Two Pillars of Integration

1

Internal ConsistencyThe Four Elements Must Align

Ensure the four elements of your proposed WHO-WHAT-HOW-VALUE framework align perfectly. Any misalignment creates vulnerability.

Key Questions:
• Does the new value proposition (WHAT) necessitate new skills (HOW) we currently lack?
• Does the new customer segment (WHO) support the new cost and revenue structure (VALUE)?
2

External ConsistencyFit the Ecosystem

Check how well your new model fits the broader business ecosystem (competitors, technology, megatrends). A model might be coherent internally but doomed if external forces tear it down.

Reality Check:
Are we swimming against unstoppable tides? Or riding massive waves?

Master Class: How Hilti Built an Unbreakable Fortress

Hilti's Fleet Management Revolution

The Challenge: Hilti sold power tools directly to construction workers. Profitable, but commoditizing. How to differentiate?

The Big Idea: Stop selling tools. Start guaranteeing "permanent tool availability."

The Total Integration Required:

WHAT Changed

Before: Selling individual tools

After: Selling guaranteed availability through fleet management service

WHO Changed

Before: Construction workers (end users)

After: CFOs of construction companies (budget controllers)

HOW Changed

Sales team: Had to negotiate multi-year service contracts instead of one-time sales

Operations: New logistics for instant tool replacement

Skillset: From product knowledge to service management

VALUE Changed

Before: One large lump sum payment

After: Regular smaller leasing payments (predictable revenue)

Required: New financial and legal structures

Why It's a Fortress: This intense interlocking made Hilti's model incredibly difficult to imitate. Competitors would need to change their WHO, WHAT, HOW, and VALUE simultaneously—a near-impossible feat.

Closing the Capability Gaps

If inconsistencies arise (e.g., missing skills in the HOW dimension), you must solve them immediately. You have three options:

1

BuildDevelop Internally

Pros: Full control, proprietary capabilities, long-term competitive advantage

Cons: Time-intensive, expensive, risk of failure

Best for: Core competencies that define your competitive edge

2

PartnerCollaborate

Pros: Faster access to missing skills, shared risk, lower upfront cost

Cons: Dependency on partners, potential conflicts, shared profits

Best for: Complementary capabilities (e.g., 3M Services outsourcing installation to partners)

3

BuyAcquire Capabilities

Pros: Fastest option, immediate access to talent/technology

Cons: Most expensive, integration challenges, cultural clashes

Best for: Critical gaps that can't wait, strategic capabilities

Your Integration Checklist

Before Proceeding to Implementation

Internal Consistency Check:

  • Do all four dimensions (WHO-WHAT-HOW-VALUE) support each other?
  • Have we identified all capability gaps in the HOW dimension?
  • Is the VALUE mechanism financially sustainable?
  • Does the new WHO (customer) actually want the new WHAT (value proposition)?

External Consistency Check:

  • Are we aligned with major megatrends or fighting them?
  • Can competitors easily copy our integrated model?
  • Does the regulatory environment support our model?
  • Are required technologies mature enough?

Capability Gap Resolution:

  • For each gap: Have we decided to Build, Partner, or Buy?
  • Do we have a realistic timeline for closing gaps?
  • Have we allocated budget for capability acquisition?

Key Takeaway

Integration is where ideas become fortresses. Achieve perfect internal consistency across WHO-WHAT-HOW-VALUE. Ensure external fit with ecosystem forces. Address capability gaps ruthlessly—build, partner, or buy. Remember Hilti: the power isn't in one clever idea, it's in the unbreakable interlocking of multiple elements. That's what makes your business model impossible to copy.
