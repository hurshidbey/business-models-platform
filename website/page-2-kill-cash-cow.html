How to Kill Your Cash Cow:
Challenging Dominant Logic

Relying on the profitability of your "cash cows" is no longer a guarantee for survival. You must become paranoid.

The Uncomfortable Reality

You must constantly question the foundations of today's success, even if things are going well.

Innovation starts with Initiation: an in-depth understanding of your entire business ecosystem. This isn't optional—it's survival.

Conduct a Critical Ecosystem Audit

Stop looking inward. True innovation requires understanding forces far beyond your immediate business.

1

Map the TerrainFar and Wide

Analyze not only your customers, partners, and competitors, but also external influencing factors like megatrends (digitalization, demographic change, resource shortages) and new technologies (AI, IoT, blockchain).

2

See the ForestFor the Trees

Include people from outside your industry in your analysis. Long-standing employees often cannot see obvious weaknesses because they are stuck in the dominant industry logic.

3

Write a EulogyFor Your Business

Even if your business is thriving, write a detailed eulogy for your current business model. This forces you to predict exactly how and why your business could fail.

4

Fight NIH SyndromeNot Invented Here

When outsiders or newcomers propose ideas, fight the instinct to reject them. Systematically analyze ideas from outside your traditional thinking patterns—they often contain seeds of revolution.

The "Write a Eulogy" Exercise

Imagine Your Business Model's Funeral

This exercise forces you to gain the necessary distance for critical analysis. Write honestly:

  • What killed us? Was it a competitor? A technology shift? Customer behavior change?
  • What were the warning signs? Which signals did we ignore?
  • What could we have done differently? When should we have acted?
  • Who disrupted us? Was it an industry outsider? A startup? Ourselves through inaction?

The Cautionary Tale of Kodak

How Kodak Invented Digital Photography—Then Died From It

The Innovation

1975: Kodak engineer Steven Sasson invented the first digital camera

Technology: Revolutionary

Market position: Dominant

→

The Failure

Decision: Shelved the technology

Reason: Couldn't abandon highly profitable analog film business

Result: Bankruptcy in 2012

The Lesson: Technological advances alone are insufficient. The true revolution lies in applying the right business model. Kodak had the technology but couldn't abandon their cash cow.

The Four Phases of Business Model Innovation

Innovation isn't random—it follows a systematic process. The 4i+ Model provides a structured approach to transform your business model from concept to reality.

Phase Core Goal Key Activities / Output
Initiation Analyze the ecosystem and gain an in-depth understanding of your current business model and environment. Map the existing WHO-WHAT-HOW-VALUE model; analyze external factors (megatrends, technologies) and critical ecosystem players (customers, competitors).
Ideation Generate new ideas and break the dominant industry logic. Systematically apply the 55+ business model patterns using the Similarity or Confrontation Principle.
Integration Build internal consistency and check the external fit of the new model. Ensure the four dimensions (WHO-WHAT-HOW-VALUE) align perfectly, addressing capability gaps and inconsistencies.
Implementation Test and optimize the new business model concept in practice. Formulate falsifiable hypotheses; conduct quick, cost-effective tests (e.g., problem/solution interviews, landing pages) to de-risk the idea.

Look Beyond Technology

Technology ≠ Innovation

Recognize that technological advances alone are insufficient. The true revolution lies in applying generic technology trends (like Blockchain or cloud computing) in a novel way—that is, applying the right business model.

Examples of technology that needed the right business model:

  • Cloud computing existed before Salesforce made it a subscription business model revolution
  • Digital music existed before iTunes/Spotify found the right business model
  • Internet existed before Google monetized it through targeted advertising
  • Smartphones existed before Apple created the App Store ecosystem

Five Steps to Break Free From Dominant Logic

  1. Map the Ecosystem: Identify all stakeholders, megatrends, and technologies affecting your industry
  2. Invite Outsiders: Bring in people from different industries to challenge your assumptions
  3. Write the Eulogy: Force yourself to envision complete failure and work backwards
  4. Welcome External Ideas: Actively seek and analyze proposals from outside your comfort zone
  5. Question the Cash Cow: Ask, "What if our most profitable product disappeared tomorrow?"

Key Takeaway

The most dangerous moment for a business is when things are going well. That's when you're most blind to disruption. Become paranoid. Question everything. Write your business model's eulogy. And remember Kodak: having the technology isn't enough—you need the courage to cannibalize your cash cow before someone else does.
